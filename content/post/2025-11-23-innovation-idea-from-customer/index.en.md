Successful innovation doesn't happen by just "sitting in an ivory tower" and brainstorming in a meeting room. It often stems from understanding the real problems of users.

Today, I have summarized key concepts from the legendary book, **"The Innovator's Toolkit,"** part of the **Harvard Business Essentials** series, along with related articles from the **Harvard Business Review (HBR)**.

The core concept is shifting the perspective from "build a product and then find customers" to "understand customers to build products that solve their needs." This is summarized in the infographic below.

## 3 Sources of "Great Ideas" from Customers

According to The Innovator's Toolkit, we don't just wait for customers to walk up and tell us. We need a process to find ideas, which is divided into 3 main approaches:

### 1. Customer Ideas (Direct Customer Input)
This is the most basic but powerful method: **"Listening."** This channel includes:
* Suggestions submitted by customers.
* Complaints (these are excellent goldmines of Pain Points).
* Talking with current customers for feedback.

### 2. Learning from Lead Users
**"Lead Users"** are a group of customers who encounter problems and try to find solutions themselves ahead of the majority of the market (e.g., people who modify sports equipment to perform better).
* **How to do it:** Observe and learn from this group, because the solutions they need today are often what the mass market will want in the future.

### 3. Empathetic Design
Sometimes customers don't know what they want or can't explain the problem. This method focuses on **"observation"** instead of asking.
* **How to do it:** Observe behavior in real-world usage environments. See how they live and where they struggle in order to unearth "Unmet Needs" that even the customers themselves might not realize.

## Key Tool: Job Mapping (Customer Job Mapping)

Once raw data is gathered from the 3 sources above, Harvard Business Review recommends a tool called **"Job Mapping"** to turn data into an action plan:

1.  **Define the 'Job':** Identify what "job" or "mission" the customer is trying to achieve (e.g., a customer doesn't want to buy a drill; they want a "hole in the wall").
2.  **Identify Pain Points & Needs:** Deconstruct that job into steps and use a magnifying glass to examine where there is friction, difficulty, or a need for help in each step.
3.  **Innovate Solutions:** Once the problems in each step are clear, then brainstorm to create innovations to solve problems at those specific points.
